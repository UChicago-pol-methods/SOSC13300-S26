## Goal

Submit your draft of the abstract, introduction, and discussion sections.

Everyone writes all three.

## Context

Your draft should fit with the combined draft of the theory, methods, and results sections shared earlier in the week.

If you want to write something that would require a change to that combined draft, do so, but clearly flag the implied change in a comment or footnote.

In this genre, readers expect the abstract, introduction, and discussion to contain specific components and to follow a fairly standardized format. Follow those conventions unless you have a strong reason not to.

## Introduction

No more than 800 words.

The introduction should introduce the topic and highlight its importance. It should then offer an abbreviated tour of the main points of the rest of the paper.

1. Briefly summarize the current state of knowledge.
2. Identify a problem, omission, inconsistency, or limitation in the literature.
3. Explain how our paper addresses that problem.
4. Summarize the main findings.
5. Highlight the broader implications.

## Discussion / conclusion

No more than 1000 words.

The discussion should give a brief summary of the findings but mainly highlight the higher-level conclusions of the paper and what those conclusions mean for policy or future research.

1. Briefly summarize the findings.
2. Emphasize their broader implications.
3. Be honest about the study's limitations.
4. Explain what remains to be done on the topic.

## Abstract

Strictly no more than 150 words.

The abstract should be a highly condensed version of the introduction.

1. Why the topic matters.
2. What prior research says.
3. What is missing or unresolved.
4. How our study addresses that gap.
5. What we find.
6. What the findings imply.
